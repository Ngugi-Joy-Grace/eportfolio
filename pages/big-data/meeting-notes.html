Deciphering Big Data

Team Meeting: Database Design Report

This was the initial meeting, and all the members were in attendance. We went through the instructions for the database design report assignment and decided that the first step was to come up with a problem statement/domain from which we will focus our report on and begin the data collection. We decided to go for a web scraping project and several domains were suggested like job market analysis by scrapping job listings websites, product price comparison, real estate listings scrapping, etc. By the meeting's conclusion, we had generated an extensive list of viable web scraping projects and compiled a set of resources related to web scraping. This information was subsequently shared on our collaborative discussion forum and the date and time for the next meeting was set.

During our second team meeting, after a thoughtful deliberation, we unanimously decided to pursue a project centred on scraping real estate listings. We recognized the importance of specificity in our project to ensure a comprehensive and in-depth analysis. With this in mind, we chose to concentrate our efforts on Manhattan's real estate market, selecting Zillow as our primary platform.

To maintain structure and clarity in our approach, we outlined key deliverables to be accomplished by our next meeting:

  1. Structure of the Report: This will provide a clear roadmap of our research, ensuring each section seamlessly leads to the next, offering a cohesive narrative. Each member was asked to come up with a structure of the report backed by research and present it in the next meeting where we would merge our ideas.
  2. GitHub Repository: We decided to set up a dedicated repository for our code to ensure transparency, ease of collaboration, and version control. This will also serve as a platform where team members can review and contribute code in a centralized manner.
  3. Data: It was emphasized that obtaining relevant and accurate data is paramount. It was decided that all team members will attempt to scrape Manhattan for-sale real estate listings data from Zillow and share their findings in the next meetings.
  4. Introduction: Having already decided on the problem statement and domain, it was decided that we start up on the introduction section of the report to set the tone and context of our research. I volunteered to do this section.

As we adjourned the meeting, each member was clear about their responsibilities, ensuring a streamlined approach leading up to our next meeting.

At our third meeting, we commenced the meeting by presenting the deliverables decided upon in our last session.

  1. Structure of the Report: The proposed structure was presented, delineating how the report would flow, from introduction to conclusion. The different sections of the report were assigned to the respective group members as well.
  2. GitHub Repository: Having set up a GitHub repository for the code, different issues had come up with the access of the repository since it was a private repository. This was addressed during the meeting to make sure all the members had access to the repository.
  3. Data: Preliminary data from Zillow regarding Manhattan real estate listings was shared. From this the important entities that would form the elements of the real estate database were decided. These were: property details, agent and agency and price history.
  4. Introduction: The drafted introduction was read out, highlighting the project's objectives, methodology, and the overarching significance of our findings in the context of Manhattan's real estate landscape. The group members were satisfied with the introduction, and it was added to the final document.

Post-presentation, while the deliverables were deemed satisfactory, a significant concern was raised regarding the data collection method. Members pointed out the limitations of traditional web scraping methods - especially considering the sheer volume and dynamic nature of real estate listings on Zillow. Traditional scraping would not only be time-consuming but also might miss out on real-time updates, thus affecting the reliability of our dataset.

After a robust discussion, we collectively decided to pivot our strategy. To enhance efficiency and accuracy, we opted to use a web scraping API named "Scrapeak." This tool promised not only faster scraping speeds but also a more streamlined process, potentially sidestepping issues like website blocks and the necessity of continuous manual updates.

The meeting concluded with tasks being redistributed considering our new approach, ensuring that each member was aligned with the next steps, especially centred around integrating and maximizing the potential of Scrapeak.

The deliverables for the next meeting were set to be an Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD) and an initial discussion into the data management pipeline.

The meeting began promptly, with an air of anticipation given the pivotal stage our project had reached. Our focus was to review the deliverables from our previous meeting and delve deeper into the heart of our database design and management.

  1. Entity Relationship Diagram (ERD): The team unveiled an ERD, visually representing the logical structure of our database. The diagram delineated the various entities identified from the real estate listings, their attributes, and the relationships between them. It offered insights into primary keys, foreign keys, and the cardinalities of relationships. The ERD was lauded for its clarity and comprehensive nature, capturing the essence of the Manhattan real estate data and how it interconnected. Some minor feedback and adjustments were discussed, with suggestions for possible refinements like having .
  2. Data Management Pipeline: A preliminary overview of our data management pipeline was presented. This overview detailed the step-by-step process of how data would flow from its point of origin on Zillow, through Scrapeak, and into our database. It touched upon the initial scraping, data cleaning and normalisation, database design, and eventual retrieval for storage. The team recognized the importance of this pipeline in ensuring data integrity, efficiency, and timeliness.

After reviewing the deliverables, our attention shifted to a crucial decision point: the choice of the database management system (DBMS). Several members presented arguments for various DBMS options, taking into consideration ease of integration with other tools and most importantly we had to consider a DBMS that was within our skillset. It was unanimously decided to go for MySQL DBMS.

The meeting concluded on a note of progress. Every member grasped the importance of the impending week, which marked our final stretch before the report submission. Recognising the urgency, it was unanimously agreed that meetings for the upcoming week on Monday, Tuesday and Sunday would be essential. Specific timings for these sessions would be determined and communicated on the days because of the differing schedules.

The meeting began with a comprehensive review of the status of the database design report. Each team member presented updates on their assigned sections. Key areas such as the introduction, objectives, data management pipeline and logical design were discussed. The importance of having relevant resources and references was highly insisted on.

The meeting commenced with the presentation of the draft for our database design report. All members actively engaged in a thorough review, pinpointing areas of strength and identifying sections that needed further refinement. A significant oversight was identified; the report lacked a concluding section. It was unanimously agreed upon that a well-structured conclusion was crucial, not only to recap the tasks undertaken in the report but also summarise the findings. As a result, drafting a conclusion was set as a priority deliverable for our next meeting on Sunday. Additionally, the importance of proofreading was emphasised. The team decided to allocate dedicated time for a collective review, ensuring that the report was free of errors and maintained a consistent structure and flow. We recognized the urgency of the tasks at hand, given the submission deadline, and resolved to intensify our efforts in the upcoming days.

For this meeting, the conclusion that was designated as a deliverable during the previous session was reviewed and edited. Following this, the team embarked on a collective proofreading session. Every section of the report was reviewed, and necessary corrections were made to ensure clarity and correction of any grammatical errors. After all the team members were satisfied with the document structure and content, the document was submitted on Turnitin to check on the similarity report. The similarity score was at 4%, within the allowable limit and the cited work was referenced. Having done all this and the document completed, the team planned to meet the next day at 10:00 pm EAT, to submit the report.

The primary agenda for this brief session was to review any last-minute feedback and officially submit the database design report. It was also agreed that the team to start researching the tasks for the executive summary report.

Tutor Meeting: Seeking Clarification

This meeting was set to address concerns over the peer review and was held with part of the team. It was a concern that the peer review might have unfairly put some team members at a disadvantage and given them lower marks. The concerns were addressed by the tutor by explaining how the peer review works and grade assignment. The tutor advised to be more intentional rating the team members.

This meeting was necessary for the tutor to offer more clarification into the feedback given. Several key factors were brought up by the tutor such as avoiding descriptive writing but rather focus on critical discussions. Questions also arose on the structure of the executive summary and how to include the technical aspects. The tutor advised that two paths can be followed: one can either include the code snippets or explain the code and actions, the preferred one being the second point.

Team Meeting: Executive Summary

This meeting was set up to discuss the feedback given by the tutor for the database design report. Several key points came up:

  1. Knowledge and Understanding: It was acknowledged that while the report was strong in theoretical aspects, it lacked a balanced application focus. The team resolved to include more real-world applications, possibly by elaborating on real estate listing scenarios where the database design was applied or would be beneficial.
  2. Application of Knowledge and Understanding: Concerns were raised about the report's theoretical objectives and the lack of clear company requirements. It was decided to revisit the objectives and align them with practical needs of a real estate company. A suggestion was made to come up with a use case study to anchor the database's practical application.
  3. Data Management Pipeline and Database Logical Design: The team agreed that a clearer flow between these sections was needed. It was decided to provide specific examples or illustrations on how stages of the data management pipeline directly impacted decisions made in the database logical design.
  4. Criticality: The team recognised the need for deeper critical analysis, especially in linking theory with practice. It was suggested to not only cite references but also to critically analyse them in relation to the practical work being done. The conclusion should also be expanded upon, offering more in-depth reflections and discussions.
  5. Structure and Presentation: The tutor noted that while the structure was deemed satisfactory, the report lacked evidence of data scraping from the website. It was decided that inclusion of snippets of the code used for scraping and possibly sample datasets to give a more comprehensive view of the work done would have been a better option. It was also noted that introduction was quoted as being too long and filled with filler words that could have otherwise been applied towards relevant content of the report.

In addition to this, initial discussion into the executive summary report structure was done and the following were decided on: a report structure defined, assigning of tasks into creation of the database and writing of the report content and the next meeting date. The meeting was adjourned and work into the executive summary report begun.

Having defined the report structure and assigned tasks to each team member, on this meeting the discussion went into the actual database implementation and what was done by the team member assigned this task. During this discussion the team member handling the database implementation was also tasked with doing the methodology as this including documenting the tasks they were doing.

An important discussion into the legal compliance issues of the database and data management pipeline was also done. It was decided that each team member would go through the relevant data protection laws that applied to the project and present their findings in the following meeting.

The meeting was adjourned, and the next meeting day set to Saturday.

This meeting involved the review of the methodology, and legal compliance issues of the database. The documents for the methodology together with the web scraping, data cleaning and database implementation code were reviewed. After critical discussion between the teammates and review, changes were suggested to the methodology which would be made and presented in the next meeting. It was requested that relevant academic journals be assembled to aid in presenting a critical discussion in the report.

The meeting was adjourned, and the teammates dispersed to tackle their respective tasks.

This meeting was set up to discuss the progress of the report and research being done. The main discussion was towards the relational database management system, it advantages and limitations. Several points were noted to be documented in a structure way in the final report. A team member volunteered to tackle this section in the discussion and the meeting was adjourned.

This meeting focused on having a checklist of all the deliverables needed for the report and planning a way to tackle the tasks not undertaken. It was noted that the discussion section of the report needed more weight in terms of academic references and a section for discussing the database needed to be added. Importantly, considering the feedback from the previous report, the content of the conclusion was decided.

This was the final meeting of the group, and it involved planning for the compiling of the report into one. One team member was assigned to ensure proper flow of the report.
